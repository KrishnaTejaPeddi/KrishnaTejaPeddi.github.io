So today I went down a rabbit hole starting with Dante's Inferno which I genuinely didn't know anything about. Turns out it's not part of the Bible which I assumed, 
just some Italian guy named Dante Alighieri who decided to write the most ambitious poem in history and somehow pulled it off. It's split into three parts — Inferno, Purgatorio 
and Paradiso — and the word Comedy doesn't mean funny, it just means the story goes from dark to light which is actually a smarter way to define it. Hell has nine circles and
they get worse as you go deeper. The logic being the more intentional the sin the worse the punishment which actually makes sense to me. Lust is at the top because its the most
human weakness, treachery is at the bottom because it requires actually choosing to destroy someone who trusted you.
Limbo was odd to me because those people didn't actually sin, they just weren't baptized. Dante himself seemed uncomfortable about it which I respect. The word limbo actually 
comes from this — meaning the edge or border — and we still use it today when something is stuck in an uncertain place which is crazy to me, one poem from 1300 and we're still
using its vocabulary.
Paolo and Francesca are my favourite story from Inferno. Two real people who fell in love reading a romance novel together, got caught by her husband who was also his brother, 
and were killed on the spot. In Hell Francesca tells the story with such tenderness that Dante actually fainted writing it. They're blown around by winds forever but they're 
together which is both romantic and devastating. The punishment mirroring the sin — driven by passion in life, driven by wind forever.
The gluttons lying in filthy slush being rained on forever is disgusting but fitting. Cerberus guards that circle which Dante borrowed from Greek mythology — Cerberus being 
the three headed dog who guards the underworld and is always hungry, appropriate for the circle of overconsumption.
The hypocrites one is my favourite punishment honestly — beautiful golden robes lined with lead on the inside. Impressive from a distance, crushing up close. Exactly like 
hypocrisy. If I go to Hell that's probably where I land.
The false prophets having their heads twisted backwards is clever too — those who presumed to see the future can now only see the past. Not just inconvenient either, 
genuinely agonizing, their tears run down their backs.
Circle 9 being frozen rather than burning is actually the most theologically interesting thing — the idea that God is warmth and love and the further you get from God the colder 
it gets. Total absence of divine warmth rather than active punishment. Satan frozen at the centre weeping and helpless is almost pitiable which I think was the point. The ultimate 
evil not as terrifying and powerful but as trapped and pathetic.
Then I went down the mythology rabbit hole through Dante. Learned about the Trojan War which started because a goddess of discord threw a golden apple at a wedding and
three goddesses fought over it and a Trojan prince had to judge who was prettiest and chose the one who offered him the most beautiful woman in the world who was 
already married to a Greek king. An entire ten year war and thousands of deaths essentially started by a wedding invitation gone wrong.
The Trojan Horse I knew vaguely but didn't know the details — the Greeks hiding inside it, the fake soldier Sinon, and then Laocoon who is now my favourite story 
from any mythology.
Laocoon was a Trojan priest who saw through the whole thing immediately. Threw a spear at the horse, made a speech warning everyone, was completely right. Then two enormous sea 
serpents came out of the sea and crushed him and his two young sons to death in front of everyone. And the Trojans interpreted this as proof the horse was sacred. The one man 
who saw the truth clearly died in a way that confirmed the lie. There's something about that story that feels true beyond mythology — it happens in smaller ways all the time. 
The person who's right gets destroyed and their destruction is used to prove them wrong.
Then Biblical stuff which as an atheist I knew basically nothing about. The Bible is split into Old and New Testament, the first five books are called the Torah or Pentateuch
meaning five books in Greek, Genesis covers creation and Adam and Eve and Cain and Abel, Exodus is Moses leading the Hebrews out of Egypt.
Cain and Abel stuck with me — God preferred Abel's offering over Cain's for reasons the text leaves deliberately ambiguous which has caused thousands of years of theological 
debate. Cain killed Abel out of jealousy and when God asked where Abel was said am I my brother's keeper which is simultaneously a lie and one of the oldest questions in human
ethics. Do we have obligations to each other simply by existing. The individualist says no. Everyone who believes in community says yes. Cain accidentally started that entire 
debate while covering up a murder.
Noah's flood has almost identical parallels in the much older Mesopotamian Epic of Gilgamesh which raises questions about where these stories actually came from and who borrowed
from whom.
Moses is one of the most human figures in all religious literature — reluctant, imperfect, frustrated, led an ungrateful people for forty years toward a promised land he never got
to enter himself. There's something genuinely sad about that.
Revelation is the strangest book in the Bible — written in coded language during Roman persecution, Babylon meaning Rome, the Beast probably meaning Nero or Domitian, the number
666 being a coded reference using a system where letters have numerical values. The four horsemen, armageddon, the whore of Babylon — all of this entered general culture so 
completely that most people use these images without knowing where they came from.
The Lamb opening the seals confused me at first — why would Christ unleash all this destruction. The answer being that in the theology of Revelation the destruction is justice for 
the oppressed rather than cruelty, and the Lamb specifically because only someone who suffered unjustly has the moral authority to correct injustice. It's a sophisticated image even
if I don't believe any of it literally. A slaughtered animal as the most powerful figure in the cosmos.
Muhammad and Islam came up through Dante placing him in Hell as a sower of discord — which Dante framed as Muhammad causing a schism from Christianity. Except Islam and Christianity 
share Abraham and both consider Jesus significant just differently, and by Dante's time Islam was already 700 years old with one of the greatest civilizations in history behind it. 
Dante was a sophisticated man who knew all this — it was a deliberate theological and political choice not ignorance.
Learned what a schism is — a formal split in a religious organization. The Great Schism of 1054 split Christianity into Catholic and Eastern Orthodox over politics, ego, and one 
single word added to a prayer. The word filioque meaning and the Son — the Western Church added it to a sacred text without asking, the Eastern Church considered this unforgivable, 
a Cardinal walked into the greatest church in the world and placed an excommunication document on the altar, and 910 years of formal separation followed. All over two words.
The seven sacraments of Catholicism I now know — Baptism, Confirmation, Eucharist, Confession, Marriage, Holy Orders, Last Rites. The Eucharist being the central one where 
Catholics
believe the bread and wine literally become the body and blood of Christ — not symbolically but actually. This doctrine called transubstantiation was one of the central disputes of
the Protestant Reformation.
Simony — buying church positions — was essentially medieval dropshipping of holy items and Dante was furious about it. The punishment being stuffed headfirst into holes in the 
ground
with flames on your feet — an inverted baptism, dead and entering punishment rather than alive entering grace.
The word sycophantic apparently comes from a Greek word meaning one who shows figs and nobody fully agrees on why that became an insult about flattery. A group of scholars have 
apparently been debating fig related insults for a very long time which is one of the more niche academic disputes I've learned about today.
Realpolitik meaning politics based purely on power rather than ethics — the strong do what they will and the weak suffer what they must. Athens said this to a small island before 
destroying it and Thucydides wrote it down and it became one of the most chilling lines in political history. Thucydides basically invented the discipline of political history 
while exiled for losing a battle which is a decent way to spend an exile.
Qohelet in Ecclesiastes concluding that everything is vanity and the wise thing to do is accept what you can't understand and enjoy simple pleasures — which sounds more Stoic and
Epicurean than Biblical and is probably why it's the most surprisingly relatable book in the whole Bible for someone like me.
